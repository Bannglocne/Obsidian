> **Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** is formally defined as the [[Market]] value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period of time. 
```
- Market Value: GDP uses market prices to add together many different types of products into a single measure of value.
- Final: To avoid double-counting, GDP only includes the value of final goods, not intermediate ones.
- Produced: GDP includes goods and services currently produced. It does not include transactions involving items produced in the past.
- Within a Country: GDP measures production that occurs within a country's borders, regardless of the nationality of the producer.
```

For an economy as a whole, _income must equal expenditure_ because every transaction has a buyer and a seller. 
GDP measures this flow of money in two ways simultaneously: 
1. By adding up the total expenditure by households 
2. By adding up the total income paid by firms.

=> GDP ($Y$) = $C$ + $I$ + $G$ + $NX$
- ==Consumption== ($C$): Spending by households on goods and services, excluding the purchase of new housing.
- ==Investment== ($I$): Spending on capital equipment, inventories, and structures, including new housing.
- ==Government Purchases== ($G$): Spending on goods and services by local, state, and federal governments (excluding _transfer payments_ like Social Security: Negative [[Tax|tax]]).
- ==Net Exports== ($NX$): Spending on domestically produced goods by foreigners (exports) minus spending on foreign goods by domestic residents (imports).

# Real vs Nominal GDP
To determine if the economy is actually producing more goods and services or if prices have simply risen, economists distinguish between two types of GDP:
- **Nominal GDP**: The production of goods and services valued at current prices.
- **Real GDP**: The production of goods and services valued at constant prices (from a designated base year). Real GDP is a better gauge of economic well-being because it shows the economy's ability to satisfy people's needs and desires.

-> The GDP Deflator: The rise in nominal GDP that is attributable to a rise in prices rather than a rise in the quantities produced.
$$\text{GDP Deflator} = \frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GD}}\times 100$$
- **[[Inflation]]**: A situation in which the economy’s overall price level is rising.
$$\text{Inflation rate in year 2} = \frac{\text{GDP deflator in year 2} - \text{GDP deflator in year 1}}{\text{GDP deflator in year 1}}\times100$$

# Is GDP a Good Measure of Well-Being?
While GDP is a good shorthand for economic health, it is not a perfect measure of happiness or quality of life.
- What it _misses_: GDP excludes the value of leisure, the quality of the environment, and activities that take place outside of markets (volunteer work, childcare at home,...).
- Why it _matters_: Despite these omissions, a large GDP is desirable because it enables a nation to afford better healthcare, educational systems, and other factors that contribute to a high standard of living.
