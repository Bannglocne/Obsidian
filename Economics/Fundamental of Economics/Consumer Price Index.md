> The **The Consumer Price Index (CPI)** measures the overall cost of the goods and services bought by a typical consumer.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) calculates it in five steps:
1. ==Fix the Basket==: Determine which products are most important to the typical consumer.
2. ==Find the Prices==: Collect data on the prices of these items over time.
3. ==Compute the Basket's Cost==: Calculate the total cost of the fixed basket for different periods.
4. ==Choose a Base Year==: Designate a benchmark year to compare others against. The index is calculated as: $$\text{CPI} = \frac{\text{Price of basket in current year}}{\text{Price of basket in base year}} \times 100$$
5. ==Compute the [[Inflation]] Rate==: Calculate the percentage change in the CPI from the previous period:  $$\text{Inflation rate in year 2} = \frac{\text{Inflation rate in year 2} - \text{Inflation rate in year 1}}{\text{Inflation rate in year 1}} \times 100$$
 _Note_: BLS also calculates the producer price index (PPI), which measures the cost of a basket of goods and services bought by firms rather than consumers.
## Problems in Measuring the Cost of Living
1. The CPI is not a perfect measure and tends to overstate the true cost of living due to three main issues.
	- ==Substitution Bias==: Consumers substitute toward goods that become relatively cheaper, but the CPI assumes they continue to buy the same "fixed basket".
	- ==Introduction of New Goods==: New products increase consumer choice and make each dollar more valuable, an effect not immediately captured by a fixed basket.
	- ==Unmeasured Quality Change==: If the quality of an item rises while its price stays the same, the value of the dollar rises, but this is difficult to measure accurately.

2. Both CPI and the **[[Gross Domestic Product#Real vs Nominal GDP|GDP Deflator]]** measure [[Inflation|inflation]], but they differ in two significant ways:
	- ==What is produced vs. bought==: The GDP deflator reflects the prices of all goods and services produced domestically, whereas the CPI reflects the prices of goods and services bought by consumers (including imports).
	- ==Fixed vs. Changing Basket==: The CPI compares the price of a fixed basket of goods to the price of that same basket in a base year, while the GDP deflator compares the price of _currently_ produced items to their price in a base year.

## Correcting Variables for Inflation
To compare dollar figures from different times, economists "deflate" them using a price index:
- Dollar Figures from Different Times:
    $$\text{Value in today's dollars} = \text{Amount in year } T \text{ dollars} \times \frac{\text{Price level today}}{\text{Price level in year } T}$$
- **Indexation:** The automatic correction of a dollar amount for the effects of inflation by law or contract (e.g., Social Security COLA).
