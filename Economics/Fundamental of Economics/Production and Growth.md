# Productivity
>**Productivity** is the quantity of goods and services produced from each unit of labor input. 

It is the fundamental driver of living standards; for a nation to enjoy a high standard of living, it must produce a large quantity of goods and services.

Productivity is determined by four main factors:
- ==Physical Capital==: The stock of equipment and structures used to produce goods and services.
- ==Human Capital==: The knowledge and skills that workers acquire through education, training, and experience.
- ==Natural Resources==: Inputs provided by nature, such as land, rivers, and mineral deposits. These can be renewable or nonrenewable .
- ==Technological Knowledge==: Society's understanding of the best ways to produce goods and services. (It differs from human capital in that it refers to the "quality of the textbooks," whereas human capital refers to the time people spend reading them.)

# Public Policy
Governments can implement various policies to raise productivity and long-run economic growth:
- ==Saving and Investment==: Because capital is a produced factor of production, society can increase its productivity by investing more current resources in the production of capital.
- ==Diminishing returns==: The property whereby the benefit from an extra unit of an input declines as the quantity of the input increases
- ==Catch-up effect==: The property whereby countries that start off poor tend to grow more rapidly than countries that start off rich
- ==Investment from Abroad==: Investment by foreigners (either through owning and operating a business or buying stock) provides a way for poor countries to learn new technologies and increase their capital stock.
- ==Education==: Investment in human capital is at least as important as investment in physical capital.
- ==Health and Nutrition==: Healthier workers are more productive.
- ==Property Rights and Political Stability==: For the _"invisible hand"_ to work, there must be an economy-wide respect for property rights so that individuals can own and control their resources without fear of theft or confiscation.
- ==Free [[Trade|trade]]==: _Outward-oriented policies_ that integrate a country into the world economy act like a technological advance by allowing a country to specialize in what it does best.
- ==Research and Development==: Most technological advances come from private research, but the government can encourage this through grants, [[Tax]] breaks, and the patent system.

# Complex Effects of Population Growth
- ==Stretching Natural Resources==
- ==Diluting the Capital Stock==: High population growth can reduce GDP per person by spreading the existing capital stock more thinly across more workers.
- ==Promoting Technological Progress==: Conversely, a larger world population may mean more scientists and inventors, leading to faster technological advances that benefit everyone.
