#economics #principles
> **Money**: The set of assets in an economy that people regularly use to buy goods and services from other people

Money has three functions in the economy:
1. **Medium of exchange**: An item that buyers give to sellers when they want to purchase goods and services
2. **Unit of account**: The yardstick people use to post prices and record debts
3. **Store of value**: An item that people can use to transfer purchasing power from the present to the future. **Liquidity**: The ease with which an asset can be converted into the economy’s medium of exchange (Money is the most liquid asset available)

Money can take two forms:
1. **Commodity money**: Money that takes the form of a commodity with intrinsic value.
2. **Fiat money**: Money without intrinsic value that is used as money because of government decree. 
	- **Currency**: The paper bills and coins in the hands of the public
	- **Demand deposits**: Balances in bank accounts that depositors can access on demand by writing a check

# The Federal Reserve System
Whenever an economy uses a system of fiat money, some agency must be responsible for regulating the system. In the US, that agency is the **Federal Reserve** (**Fed**). Fed is the **central bank** of the US - an institution designed to oversee the banking system and regulate the quantity of money in the economy.
- Structure: It is run by a Board of Governors with seven members and includes 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks.
- Monetary Policy: The Fed controls the **money supply**—the quantity of money available in the economy—primarily through the **Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC)**.
- Primary Tool: The Fed's main tool is **open-market operations**, where it buys government bonds to increase the money supply or sells them to decrease it.

## The Fed’s Tools of Monetary Control
The Fed has several ways to influence the quantity of money:
- **Influencing Reserves:** It can change reserves via **open-market operations** or by lending to banks through the **discount window** (charging the **discount rate**).
- **Influencing the Reserve Ratio:** It can change **reserve requirements** (the minimum reserves banks must hold) or the interest rate it pays banks on their reserves.

# Banks and the Money Supply
Banks influence the money supply through a system called **fractional-reserve banking**, where they hold only a fraction of their deposits as reserves and lend out the rest.
- **Money Creation:** When a bank makes a loan, it creates money. 
- **The Money Multiplier:** This is the amount of money the banking system generates with each dollar of reserves. It is the reciprocal of the **reserve ratio** ($1/R$). If the reserve ratio is $a\%$, the money multiplier is $a$.
