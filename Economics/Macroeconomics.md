# Measuring a Nation’s Income
> **Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** is formally defined as the market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period of time. 
```
- Market Value: GDP uses market prices to add together many different types of products into a single measure of value.
- Final: To avoid double-counting, GDP only includes the value of final goods, not intermediate ones.
- Produced: GDP includes goods and services currently produced. It does not include transactions involving items produced in the past.
- Within a Country: GDP measures production that occurs within a country's borders, regardless of the nationality of the producer.
```

For an economy as a whole, _income must equal expenditure_ because every transaction has a buyer and a seller. 
GDP measures this flow of money in two ways simultaneously: 
1. By adding up the total expenditure by households 
2. By adding up the total income paid by firms.

=> GDP ($Y$) = $C$ + $I$ + $G$ + $NX$
- ==Consumption== ($C$): Spending by households on goods and services, excluding the purchase of new housing.
- ==Investment== ($I$): Spending on capital equipment, inventories, and structures, including new housing.
- ==Government Purchases== ($G$): Spending on goods and services by local, state, and federal governments (excluding _transfer payments_ like Social Security: Negative [[Basic Economics#Taxes|tax]]).
- ==Net Exports== ($NX$): Spending on domestically produced goods by foreigners (exports) minus spending on foreign goods by domestic residents (imports).

## Real versus Nominal GDP
To determine if the economy is actually producing more goods and services or if prices have simply risen, economists distinguish between two types of GDP:
- **Nominal GDP**: The production of goods and services valued at current prices.
- **Real GDP**: The production of goods and services valued at constant prices (from a designated base year). Real GDP is a better gauge of economic well-being because it shows the economy's ability to satisfy people's needs and desires.

-> The GDP Deflator: The rise in nominal GDP that is attributable to a rise in prices rather than a rise in the quantities produced.
$$\text{GDP Deflator} = \frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GD}}\times 100$$
**Inflation**: A situation in which the economy’s overall price level is rising.
$$\text{Inflation rate in year 2} = \frac{\text{GDP deflator in year 2} - \text{GDP deflator in year 1}}{\text{GDP deflator in year 1}}\times100$$
## Is GDP a Good Measure of Well-Being?
While GDP is a good shorthand for economic health, it is not a perfect measure of happiness or quality of life.
- What it _misses_: GDP excludes the value of leisure, the quality of the environment, and activities that take place outside of markets (volunteer work, childcare at home,...).
- Why it _matters_: Despite these omissions, a large GDP is desirable because it enables a nation to afford better healthcare, educational systems, and other factors that contribute to a high standard of living.

# Measuring the Cost of Living
> The **The Consumer Price Index (CPI)** measures the overall cost of the goods and services bought by a typical consumer.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) calculates it in five steps:
1. ==Fix the Basket==: Determine which products are most important to the typical consumer.
2. ==Find the Prices==: Collect data on the prices of these items over time.
3. ==Compute the Basket's Cost==: Calculate the total cost of the fixed basket for different periods.
4. ==Choose a Base Year==: Designate a benchmark year to compare others against. The index is calculated as: $$\text{CPI} = \frac{\text{Price of basket in current year}}{\text{Price of basket in base year}} \times 100$$
5. ==Compute the Inflation Rate==: Calculate the percentage change in the CPI from the previous period:  $$\text{Inflation rate in year 2} = \frac{\text{Inflation rate in year 2} - \text{Inflation rate in year 1}}{\text{Inflation rate in year 1}} \times 100$$
 _Note_: BLS also calculates the producer price index (PPI), which measures the cost of a basket of goods and services bought by firms rather than consumers.
## Problems in Measuring the Cost of Living
1. The CPI is not a perfect measure and tends to overstate the true cost of living due to three main issues.
	- ==Substitution Bias==: Consumers substitute toward goods that become relatively cheaper, but the CPI assumes they continue to buy the same "fixed basket".
	- ==Introduction of New Goods==: New products increase consumer choice and make each dollar more valuable, an effect not immediately captured by a fixed basket.
	- ==Unmeasured Quality Change==: If the quality of an item rises while its price stays the same, the value of the dollar rises, but this is difficult to measure accurately.

2. Both CPI and the **GDP Deflator** measure inflation, but they differ in two significant ways:
	- ==What is produced vs. bought==: The GDP deflator reflects the prices of all goods and services produced domestically, whereas the CPI reflects the prices of goods and services bought by consumers (including imports).
	- ==Fixed vs. Changing Basket==: The CPI compares the price of a fixed basket of goods to the price of that same basket in a base year, while the GDP deflator compares the price of _currently_ produced items to their price in a base year.

## Correcting Variables for Inflation
To compare dollar figures from different times, economists "deflate" them using a price index:
- Dollar Figures from Different Times:
    $$\text{Value in today's dollars} = \text{Amount in year } T \text{ dollars} \times \frac{\text{Price level today}}{\text{Price level in year } T}$$
- **Indexation:** The automatic correction of a dollar amount for the effects of inflation by law or contract (e.g., Social Security COLA).

=> To understand how much a person earns in a savings account, we need to consider both the interest rate and the change in the prices.
$$\text{Real interest rate} = \text{Nominal interest rate} - \text{Inflation rate}$$
```
- The nominal interest rate: The rate as usually reported
- The real interest rate is corrected for inflation
```

# Production and Growth
## Productivity
>**Productivity** is the quantity of goods and services produced from each unit of labor input. 

It is the fundamental driver of living standards; for a nation to enjoy a high standard of living, it must produce a large quantity of goods and services.

Productivity is determined by four main factors:
- ==Physical Capital==: The stock of equipment and structures used to produce goods and services.
- ==Human Capital==: The knowledge and skills that workers acquire through education, training, and experience.
- ==Natural Resources==: Inputs provided by nature, such as land, rivers, and mineral deposits. These can be renewable or nonrenewable .
- ==Technological Knowledge==: Society's understanding of the best ways to produce goods and services. (It differs from human capital in that it refers to the "quality of the textbooks," whereas human capital refers to the time people spend reading them.)

## Public Policy
Governments can implement various policies to raise productivity and long-run economic growth:
- ==Saving and Investment==: Because capital is a produced factor of production, society can increase its productivity by investing more current resources in the production of capital.
- ==Diminishing returns==: The property whereby the benefit from an extra unit of an input declines as the quantity of the input increases
- ==Catch-up effect==: The property whereby countries that start off poor tend to grow more rapidly than countries that start off rich
- ==Investment from Abroad==: Investment by foreigners (either through owning and operating a business or buying stock) provides a way for poor countries to learn new technologies and increase their capital stock.
- ==Education==: Investment in human capital is at least as important as investment in physical capital.
- ==Health and Nutrition==: Healthier workers are more productive.
- ==Property Rights and Political Stability==: For the _"invisible hand"_ to work, there must be an economy-wide respect for property rights so that individuals can own and control their resources without fear of theft or confiscation.
- ==Free Trade==: _Outward-oriented policies_ that integrate a country into the world economy act like a technological advance by allowing a country to specialize in what it does best.
- ==Research and Development==: Most technological advances come from private research, but the government can encourage this through grants, tax breaks, and the patent system.

## Population Growth
Population growth has complex effects on productivity:
- ==Stretching Natural Resources==
- ==Diluting the Capital Stock==: High population growth can reduce GDP per person by spreading the existing capital stock more thinly across more workers.
- ==Promoting Technological Progress==: Conversely, a larger world population may mean more scientists and inventors, leading to faster technological advances that benefit everyone.
