#maths #calculus 
> Let $f$ be a [[one-to-one function]] with domain $A$ and range $B$. Then its **inverse function** $f^{-1}$ has domain $B$ and range $A$ and is defined by

$$
f^{-1}(y) = x \Leftrightarrow f(x) = y
$$
for any $y$ in $B$

cancellation equations: 
$$
f^{-1}(f(x)) = x \text { for every x in A}
$$
$$
f(f^{-1}(x)) = x \text { for every x in B}
$$


> [!Note] Theorem
> If $f$ is a [[one-to-one function|one-to-one]] [[continuity|continuous]] [[function]] defined on an interval, then its inverse [[function]] $f^{-1}$ is also [[continuity|continuous]].


> [!NOTE] Theorem
> If $f$ is a [[one-to-one function|one-to-one]] [[derivatives|differentiable]] [[function]] with inverse [[function]] $f^{-1}$ and $f'(f^{-1}(a)) \ne 0$ , then the inverse [[function]] is [[derivatives|differentiable]] at $a$ and

$$
(f^{-1})'(a) = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(a))}
$$
