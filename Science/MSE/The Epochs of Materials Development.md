
#material #history
- **The Stone Age (Prehistory – ~3000 BCE):**  _Stone (flint, chert), wood, bone, and clay._
    
    - Humans relied on materials found in nature with minimal chemical alteration. Shaping was done entirely through physical force (chipping, grinding).
        
- **The Bronze Age (~3000 BCE – ~1200 BCE):**  _Copper and its alloy, Bronze (copper + tin)._
    
    - The dawn of metallurgy. Humans learned to smelt ores and discovered that alloying tin with copper produced a significantly harder and more durable metal, revolutionizing agriculture, warfare, and trade.
        
- **The Iron Age (~1200 BCE – ~1000 CE):**  _Iron._
    
    - Iron smelting required much higher furnace temperatures than copper. Because iron ore is vastly more abundant than tin, iron tools became cheaper and more widely available, "democratizing" agriculture and warfare.
        
- **The Steel/Industrial Age (18th Century – Mid-20th Century):**  _Steel, early polymers (rubber, Bakelite)._
    
    - Inventions like the Bessemer process allowed for the cheap, mass production of steel. This high-tensile material made the Industrial Revolution possible, paving the way for railways, steam engines, and skyscrapers.
        
- **The Silicon/Information Age (Mid-20th Century – Present):**  _Silicon, advanced composites, synthetic plastics._
    
    - The focus shifted from mechanical strength to electronic, magnetic, and optical properties. The mastery of silicon semiconductors enabled the microchip, giving rise to modern computing and the digital revolution.
        
- **The Nano/Synthetic Age (21st Century – Future):**  _Graphene, carbon nanotubes, metamaterials, biomaterials._
    
    - The current frontier. Instead of finding materials to suit our needs, we now engineer them from the bottom up—atom by atom—to exhibit specific, often unnatural, properties (e.g., negative refractive index, extreme strength-to-weight ratios).
